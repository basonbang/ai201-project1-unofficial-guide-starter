Course Information
Operating System Principles
Class Number: CSC 0415 Sections 01, 02, 03     
Semester: Fall 2025

Description

Prerequisites: CSC 256, CSC 340, MATH 324, and PHYS 230 with grades of C or better or graduate standing. Operating system concepts: concurrent processes, basic synchronization techniques, deadlock, memory management, file systems, security, and distributed processing. Extra fee required.

CSC-415 is a difficult and challenging course that draws on knowledge learned about binary and hexadecimal numbering systems, machine instructions, data and functionality encapsulation, writing approach and results, mathematics skills, and programming skills.  The course will utilize the C programming language in a Ubuntu Linux environment for the entire semester.

Course work is cumulative with assignments building on each other and the skills learned applied in the term group project.

Student Learning Outcomes:
Introduce the student to Operating System types and concepts.
Cover processes, threads, process synchronization, inter-process communications, CPU scheduling, memory management, virtual memory, mass storage, file systems, and device drivers.
Understanding the difference and purpose between real-time and multi-tasking systems
Introduce the concepts physical computing and how devices and other hardware interact with the computer and operating system.
Discuss correct synchronization techniques for both application programs and kernel code running on various platforms.
How to approach large project solutions.  Breaking problems down.
Working in teams and communication.
Functional encapsulation and interfaces.
Documentation and explanation of approach to problems.

Policies
Home Work Policy
Homework is due at the assigned date and time.  Homework may be turned in up to 4 days late, with each day reducing your maximum grade by 5%

When Submitted the Maximum Grade is: 

If the homework is submitted On Time the maximum grade is 100%
If the homework is less than 24 hours late the maximum grade is 95%
If the homework is at or more than 24 hours late but less than 48 hours late the maximum grade is 90%
If the homework is at or more than 48 hours late but less than 72 hours late the maximum grade is 85%
If the homework is at or more than 72 hours late but less than 96 hours late the maximum grade is 80%
If the homework is at or more than 96 hours late the grade is a 0%.
 

Assignment Submission & Grading
If for any reason your application fails to compile with make (this includes, but not limited to failures to update the Makefile, incorrect file names, compilation errors, etc.) there will be a minimum 25% penalty

Failure to submit you assignment (the required material, usually the writeup PDF) to Canvas results in no submission and a grade of 0 for the assignment, regardless of what is submitted in github.

No changes to github are allowed after you submit to Canvas.  That submission to Canvas is an indication that your assignment is complete.

Attendance and Participation
Attendance and participation are critical in this class and is a requirement and accounts for 10% of your grade.

This course has both asynchronous components as well as synchronous - live in-person classes.  There are activity checks, you are expected to complete them as specified and to attend the class meetings.  You need to be an active participant in the class, that means asking question, responding promptly to in class questions and contributing in forums and group assignments.

This also includes your group projects, it is your responsibility to stay in touch and work with your team.

Since we are back in person, remote attendance will only be allowed on a limited and exceptional basis and must be requested in advance.  Failure to attend in person can result in you being dropped from the course.

Attending the class via Zoom will only be allowed for approved scheduled absences (such as attending a conference representing SFSU).   Such requests must be made at least 1 week in advance.  Lateness, illness, transportation issues in which you do not attend in person will be marked as absent and you will have the opportunity to confer with your classmates and/or watch the recorded videos after the class.

Plagiarism Policy
In this course, we will be employing software from TurnItIn.com as a tool for learning how to correctly cite resources. Turnitin.com is useful in helping to determine whether or not a writer is relying too heavily upon sources and whether a writer has given appropriate credit to an author or source. Students will be assigned a username and will be expected to submit their papers on the assigned due date to Turnitin.com, which in turn produces an “originality” report. This report will compare the student’s paper to content on the Web, to the Turnitin.com database of previously submitted papers, and to databases of common full-text journals. In creating an account, students agree that by taking this course, all required papers will be reviewed by Turnitin.com and acknowledge that their papers will be included as source documents in the Turnitin.com reference database solely for the purpose of detecting plagiarism. All identifiers (student name, ID #, etc.) will be removed from the paper before it is uploaded into Turnitin.com. Use of Turnitin.com services is subject to the Terms and Conditions of Use, which are posted on the Turnitin.com website.

Copying code from another student, the internet, using ChatGPT or similar AI systems to generate code or purporting that code you did not write as your own are all examples of plagiarism.

In addition the Computer Science plagiarism policy is part of this syllabus https://cs.sfsu.edu/undergrads/student-policiesLinks to an external site.

If you are suspected of plagiarism, you will receive a grade of 1 for an assignment.  In addition you may also be reported to the CS Department and to the Office of Student Conduct.   You can setup office hours if you wish to dispute the grade.  You have 10 days from the time the grades are posted on an assignment to meet with me to challenge the grade.

Assignments and exams are individual (unless explicitly stated otherwise), but we encourage collaboration with course staff and classmates.  However, you must be careful how you collaborate.  Use the table below as a reference and guideline.
